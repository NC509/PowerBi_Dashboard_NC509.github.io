## Analysis
From background research we know that temperature levels over 30 C° on average create a risk that leads to the coral bleaching process. In the analysis view we can see the change of temperature overtime relative to that bleaching risk zone. Additionally, when changing the visual to the "Average Temperature C° and Percent Bleaching by Year" view, we can now witness the relationship of the average temperature change and average percent of coral bleaching. This different view is shown below.

#### Example:
Let's look at a time where there were coral bleaching risk. In this example we want to look at the Atlantic ocean, which only shows the Tropical Atlantic region, from years 2010 - 2020. In this point the average temperature is above the danger threshold showing there is likely a chance for coral bleaching. When selecting the year 2015 we can see there was an average of 34.52% bleaching to this area while the temperature was 30.90 C° on average. This bleaching affected different substrate types within that region in this year. Now looking at the global view during that year, we see 19.14% of bleaching within that Ocean Region on average for that year. This doesn't sound like a significant amount of coral bleaching, but this is an impactful amount. When coral bleaches, this causes problems for the marine life surrounding it, especially in longterm stressful conditions where the coral cannot reverse back to it's colorful state.

## Why is this important?
Coral bleaching is a product of a defense mechanism activated by coral. Typically, algae will attach to coral creating a symbiotic relationship where the coral receives nutrients from the algae and the algae have a safe place to grow. As water temperature rises, algae begin to excrete toxic chemicals instead of nutrients. The coral then reacts by expelling the algae, which results in the coral taking on a greyish white color. Without their food source, the coral begins to die. (Ashlee 2025)
 
Coral reefs have a significant role in the global environment as they are one of the most biodiverse ecosystems. Over 25% of all marine life live in coral reefs. According to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, “the total economic value of coral reefs in the U.S. alone is $3.4 billion. Coral species loss also has the potential to impact medicine discovery/development and shoreline protection. The food supply is also affected for coastal communities that depend on the animals coral reefs provide." (PBS, 2024) "Bleaching is often caused by wide changes in temperature as coral are very delicate. As the summer months happen in both hemispheres, coral bleaching increases, the coral recovers in the winter months. A change of 3 degrees Celsius in water temperature makes a vibrant ecosystem to a ghost-town."(PBS, 2024) 
 
The climate plays such a large role in the effects of the coral reef. According to PBS, they express, “In January 2010, ocean temperatures in the Florida Keys dropped 6.7° C lower than average, causing a bleaching event that led to coral mortality. The cold temperature levels can be a problem, as well. Other potential causes include: Runoff and pollution. Overexposure to sunlight. Extreme low tides.” (PBS, 2024). With temperature being the lead factor, these are some other possible contributors to this process. This brings the argument if cold temperature makes as large of an impact just as the heat does. With this in mind the main focus for this project will be on temperature, but this is an aspect that needs to be recognized. This is important to recognize since the bleaching of coral reefs around the world affects human life as well. If there is room for humans to make a change to better the health of the coral reef it should be recognized. 

 Lastly, coral bleaching isn't a recent phenomena, in fact currently we are looking into being in the 4th global coral bleaching event according to the NOAA. This has been an ongoing coral bleaching event and will be the biggest to date when compared to the last events. Within this event 84.4% of the world’s coral reefs are experiencing bleaching level heat stress. The 3rd event occurred from 2014-2017 with 68.2% of the world’s coral experiencing bleaching level heat stress. The 1st and 2nd event was in 1998 and 2010. (NOAA and Coral Reef Watch, 2025).
